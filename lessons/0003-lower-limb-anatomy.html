Lesson 0003

Lower Limb Anatomy

MRCEM Primary · Phase 1 Foundations · Week 2

🗺️ MRCEM Blueprint Context: Lower Limb Anatomy represents ~8–10 of the 60 Anatomy questions (~5% of total exam). Combined with Upper Limb (~8–10Q), these are the highest-yield anatomy subcategories. Lower limb nerve lesions — especially common peroneal nerve (foot drop) and sciatic nerve — are tested in every MRCEM Primary sitting. You cannot afford to lose marks here.

Why This Matters

Every shift brings lower limb pathology: the hip fracture at 3am, the dislocated knee with a threatened popliteal artery, the drunk who can't dorsiflex their foot. You cannot assess a tibial plateau fracture without understanding the common peroneal nerve. You cannot reduce a hip dislocation without respecting the sciatic nerve. MRCEM Primary tests lower limb anatomy as applied clinical knowledge — nerve lesion vignettes, fracture complications, and gait abnormalities are board favourites.

Knowledge: The Core Framework

1. The Lumbosacral Plexus — HIGHEST-YIELD TOPIC

Like the brachial plexus in the upper limb, the lumbosacral plexus is tested in every sitting. It supplies all motor and sensory innervation to the lower limb. Divided into lumbar plexus (L1–L4) and sacral plexus (L4–S4).

Lumbar Plexus (L1–L4) — Anterior Thigh

NerveRootsKey Motor SupplySensoryClinical
Femoral n. L2, L3, L4 Anterior thigh: quadriceps (knee extension), sartorius, pectineus Anterior thigh + medial leg/ankle (saphenous n.) Injury → weak knee extension + absent knee jerk. Psoas abscess, pelvic surgery, femoral artery catheterisation
Obturator n. L2, L3, L4 Medial thigh: adductors (adductor longus/brevis/magnus, gracilis, obturator externus) Medial thigh Injury → weak hip adduction. Pelvic surgery, obturator hernia
Lateral femoral cutaneous n. L2, L3 None (pure sensory) Lateral thigh Meralgia paraesthetica — entrapment under inguinal ligament → burning lateral thigh pain

Sacral Plexus (L4–S4) — Posterior Thigh + Everything Below Knee

NerveRootsKey Motor SupplySensoryClinical
Sciatic n. L4–S3 Hamstrings (via tibial division) + all muscles below knee (via tibial + common peroneal divisions) Posterior thigh, entire leg and foot (except medial leg/ankle — saphenous n.) Posterior hip dislocation (most common). Piriformis syndrome. Largest nerve in the body — exits pelvis through greater sciatic foramen BELOW piriformis
Superior gluteal n. L4, L5, S1 Gluteus medius + minimus, tensor fasciae latae None (pure motor) Injury → Trendelenburg gait (pelvis drops on unsupported side). Intramuscular injection too high/medial
Inferior gluteal n. L5, S1, S2 Gluteus maximus (hip extension) None (pure motor) Injury → difficulty rising from chair, climbing stairs
Pudendal n. S2, S3, S4 External anal + urethral sphincters, pelvic floor Perineum Pudendal nerve entrapment in Alcock's canal. S2–S4 keeps the pee off the floor
🔥 Quick recall: Name the major nerves of the lumbar plexus and sacral plexus
Lumbar plexus (L1–L4): Femoral n., Obturator n., Lateral femoral cutaneous n.
Sacral plexus (L4–S4): Sciatic n. (L4–S3), Superior gluteal n., Inferior gluteal n., Pudendal n. (S2–S4)
Sciatic = tibial division + common peroneal division — they separate in the distal posterior thigh/popliteal fossa

2. The Hip Joint — Stability and Blood Supply

The hip is a ball-and-socket joint (femoral head + acetabulum). It sacrifices mobility for stability — unlike the shoulder.

FeatureDetailsClinical Significance
Blood supply to femoral head Medial + lateral circumflex femoral arteries (from profunda femoris) → retinacular arteries → artery of ligamentum teres (from obturator a., minor contribution) Intracapsular femoral neck fracture → retinacular vessels torn → avascular necrosis of femoral head. The artery of ligamentum teres is insufficient to sustain the head alone
Hip dislocation Posterior = 90% (dashboard injury, shortened + internally rotated leg). Anterior = 10% (externally rotated + abducted) Posterior dislocation → sciatic nerve at risk. Common peroneal division most vulnerable (lateral, more superficial). Document foot dorsiflexion before AND after reduction
Stability Bony (deep acetabulum), ligamentous (iliofemoral — strongest ligament in body), muscular (glutei, short external rotators) Hip dislocation requires significant force (high-energy trauma, except prosthetic hips). Native hip = hard to dislocate; THR = easier
🧠 Exam pattern: "An elderly woman falls and sustains an intracapsular femoral neck fracture. She undergoes hemiarthroplasty. Which vessel was disrupted, and why is AVN a concern?" The answer is the retinacular branches of the medial circumflex femoral artery. The artery of ligamentum teres contributes minimally and atrophies with age. This is tested in every MRCEM sitting — know it cold.

🔬 Recalled Exam Pattern: The exam loves asking "Which muscle in the posterior thigh is NOT innervated by the tibial division of the sciatic nerve?" Answer: short head of biceps femoris (common peroneal division). This is the lower limb equivalent of "FCU is ulnar nerve" — a single exception that gets tested repeatedly. Similarly: "Which adductor receives dual innervation?" → adductor magnus.

4. The Knee Joint and Popliteal Fossa

Popliteal Fossa — Contents (Medial to Lateral)

🧠 Mnemonic: Serve And Volley Next Ball
Semimembranosus → Artery (popliteal) → Vein (popliteal) → Nerve (tibial) → Biceps femoris

⚡ Popliteal fossa boundaries:
Superomedial: Semimembranosus + semitendinosus
Superolateral: Biceps femoris
Inferomedial: Medial head of gastrocnemius
Inferolateral: Lateral head of gastrocnemius + plantaris
Floor: Popliteal surface of femur, posterior capsule of knee joint, popliteus muscle
Roof: Deep fascia + small saphenous vein + posterior femoral cutaneous nerve

Knee Ligaments — Unhappy Triad

LigamentAttachmentsFunctionInjury Mechanism
ACL Anterior intercondylar area of tibia → medial aspect of lateral femoral condyle Prevents anterior translation of tibia on femur Non-contact pivoting injury, hyperextension. Lachman test > anterior drawer
PCL Posterior intercondylar area of tibia → lateral aspect of medial femoral condyle Prevents posterior translation of tibia Dashboard injury — tibia driven posteriorly. Posterior sag sign
MCL Medial femoral epicondyle → medial tibial condyle Resists valgus stress Valgus force to knee. Attached to medial meniscus
LCL Lateral femoral epicondyle → head of fibula Resists varus stress Varus force. Not attached to lateral meniscus
⚡ The Unhappy Triad (O'Donoghue's Triad): ACL + MCL + Medial meniscus. Mechanism: valgus force + external rotation of tibia on a fixed foot — classic football/skiing injury. The MCL is attached to the medial meniscus, so valgus stress injures both. The ACL tears from rotational strain. The LCL is NOT attached to the lateral meniscus — another exam favourite.

Tibialis posterior · Digitorum longus · Artery (posterior tibial) · Vein · Nerve (tibial) · Hallucis longus
Deep to the flexor retinaculum, posterior to the medial malleolus.

6. The Ankle and Foot

Ankle Ligaments

SideLigament ComplexComponentsInjury
Lateral Lateral ligament complex ATFL (anterior talofibular — weakest, most commonly injured)
CFL (calcaneofibular)
PTFL (posterior talofibular — strongest)		 Inversion injury (rolling ankle). ATFL → CFL → PTFL in order of injury severity
Medial Deltoid ligament 4 parts: tibionavicular, tibiocalcaneal, anterior + posterior tibiotalar Eversion injury. Much stronger than lateral — eversion injury more likely to cause medial malleolus fracture than isolated ligament rupture
🧠 Exam pattern: "A 25-year-old footballer inverts his ankle during a tackle. Which ligament is MOST likely injured?" → ATFL (anterior talofibular ligament). The exam expects you to know the order: ATFL first, then CFL, then PTFL. The Ottawa ankle rules are MRCEM clinical content but the anatomy basis (ATFL tenderness = lateral malleolus tip) is the Primary connection.

Foot — Arches and Support

The foot has three arches (medial longitudinal, lateral longitudinal, transverse) maintained by bony configuration, ligaments (spring ligament = plantar calcaneonavicular — most important for medial arch), and dynamic support (tibialis posterior, peroneus longus, intrinsic foot muscles).

⚡ High-yield fact: Tibialis posterior dysfunction = acquired flat foot (pes planus) in adults. The tibialis posterior tendon is the primary dynamic stabiliser of the medial longitudinal arch. Rupture or tendinopathy → progressive collapse of the arch → valgus hindfoot deformity. Common in middle-aged women, often mistaken for ankle sprain.

7. Lower Limb Nerve Lesions — THE HIGHEST-YIELD SECTION

MRCEM Primary loves lower limb nerve lesion questions. You must know the classic presenting signs, anatomical level of injury, and distinguishing features.

NerveClassic DeformityMotor LossSensory LossCommon Scenarios
Common peroneal n. Foot drop + high-stepping gait Dorsiflexors (tibialis anterior) + evertors (peronei). Toe extensors. Foot slap when walking Dorsum of foot (superficial peroneal) + first web space (deep peroneal) Fibular neck fracture, tight plaster cast below knee, prolonged leg crossing, lithotomy position. Most common lower limb mononeuropathy
Tibial n. Loss of plantarflexion, cannot stand on toes All posterior leg muscles + intrinsic foot muscles (claw toes) Sole of foot (medial + lateral plantar nerves) Knee dislocation (popliteal fossa), tarsal tunnel syndrome
Sciatic n. Flail foot + weak knee flexion Hamstrings + everything below knee. Foot drop + loss of plantarflexion Posterior thigh, entire leg and foot (except medial leg/ankle — saphenous n.) Posterior hip dislocation, intramuscular injection (wrong quadrant). Common peroneal division more affected than tibial (more superficial)
Femoral n. Cannot extend knee. Buckling when walking downstairs Quadriceps → weak knee extension, sartorius Anterior thigh + medial leg/ankle (saphenous n.) Pelvic surgery, psoas abscess, femoral artery catheterisation. Absent knee jerk (L3, L4)
Obturator n. Weak hip adduction. Waddling gait Adductor muscles Medial thigh Pelvic surgery, obturator hernia. Rare in isolation
Superior gluteal n. Trendelenburg gait — pelvis drops on opposite side when standing on affected leg Gluteus medius + minimus → cannot abduct hip when contralateral foot is off ground None (pure motor) Intramuscular injection in superomedial quadrant of buttock. Trendelenburg test positive
⚡ Key discriminator — Foot drop:
Common peroneal nerve at fibular neck: Foot drop ONLY (dorsiflexors and evertors). Plantarflexion intact, ankle jerk intact (tibial n., S1, S2). The most common cause of foot drop.
Sciatic nerve: Foot drop + loss of plantarflexion + weak hamstrings. Ankle jerk absent. Much rarer.
L5 radiculopathy: Foot drop + weak big toe extension (EHL) + sensory loss lateral leg and dorsum of foot. Plantarflexion and ankle jerk intact (S1). Back pain often present. Classic exam question: "Is it L5 root or common peroneal nerve?"
🔥 Quick recall: Trendelenburg test — which nerve is affected, and describe the test
Superior gluteal nerve (L4, L5, S1) → gluteus medius + minimus.
Test: Patient stands on one leg. Normally, the gluteus medius on the standing side contracts to keep the pelvis level. If the nerve/muscle is weak, the contralateral pelvis drops (Trendelenburg sign positive).
Compensation: Patient leans trunk toward the affected side to shift centre of gravity (compensated Trendelenburg gait).

8. Blood Supply to the Lower Limb

External iliac a. (passes under inguinal ligament) → Femoral a. → Popliteal a. (through adductor hiatus) → bifurcates at lower border of popliteus into Anterior tibial a. + Posterior tibial a. (continues as Peroneal/Fibular a.).
Dorsalis pedis a. = continuation of anterior tibial a. on dorsum of foot. Posterior tibial a. passes behind medial malleolus (Tom, Dick, And Very Nervous Harry).
⚡ The femoral triangle (NAVEL — lateral to medial):
Nerve (femoral), Artery (femoral), Vein (femoral), Empty space, Lymphatics (canal of Rosenmüller).
Boundaries: SAIL: Sartorius (lateral), Adductor longus (medial), Inguinal Ligament (superior). Floor: iliopsoas + pectineus.
🧠 Exam pattern: The exam may ask about the dorsalis pedis pulse — it's located between the first and second metatarsals, lateral to the extensor hallucis longus tendon. Absence may indicate peripheral arterial disease or be congenital (~10% absent). The posterior tibial pulse is behind the medial malleolus. Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI) is tested in MRCEM Primary EBM/physiology.

Skills: Retrieval Practice

Answer each question from memory before clicking. These are constructed to real MRCEM difficulty — clinical vignettes testing nerve localisation and anatomical reasoning.

Q1. A 32-year-old man sustains a fracture of the right fibular neck while playing football. Three weeks after cast removal, he complains that his right foot "slaps" when he walks and he cannot clear his foot from the ground during the swing phase of gait. On examination, he cannot dorsiflex his right foot or extend his toes. Eversion of the foot is also weak. Sensation is reduced over the dorsum of the foot and the first web space. However, plantarflexion is strong and the ankle jerk is intact. Which nerve is injured, and at what level?

A. Tibial nerve — compressed in the tarsal tunnel at the medial malleolus
B. Common peroneal nerve — compressed at the fibular neck
C. Sciatic nerve — injured in the gluteal region
D. L5 nerve root — compressed by a prolapsed intervertebral disc
E. Deep peroneal nerve — compressed under the inferior extensor retinaculum

Q2. A 28-year-old driver is brought to the ED following a head-on collision. His right hip is flexed, internally rotated, and adducted — a classic posterior hip dislocation. After successful closed reduction under sedation, he is noted to have weakness of dorsiflexion AND plantarflexion of the right foot, with absent ankle jerk but intact knee jerk. Sensation is reduced over the entire foot and posterior thigh. Which nerve has been injured?

A. Femoral nerve — compressed by the femoral head anteriorly
B. Common peroneal nerve — the most lateral structure in the popliteal fossa
C. Sciatic nerve — stretched or compressed by the posteriorly displaced femoral head
D. Obturator nerve — running through the obturator canal
E. Superior gluteal nerve — exiting through the greater sciatic foramen

Q3. A junior doctor is performing a femoral venous blood gas in a hypotensive patient. They palpate the femoral artery just below the inguinal ligament and insert the needle immediately medial to the pulsation. From lateral to medial, the structures within the femoral sheath are arranged in which order (NAVEL)?

A. Artery → Nerve → Vein → Lymphatics (ANVL)
B. Vein → Artery → Nerve → Lymphatics (VANL)
C. Nerve → Artery → Vein → Lymphatics (NAVEL)
D. Artery → Vein → Nerve → Lymphatics (AVNL)
E. Lymphatics → Vein → Artery → Nerve (LVAN)

Q4. All of the following muscles in the posterior compartment of the thigh are innervated by the TIBIAL division of the sciatic nerve EXCEPT:

A. Semitendinosus — forming the superomedial border of the popliteal fossa
B. Semimembranosus — the deepest of the hamstring muscles
C. Short head of biceps femoris — the lateral hamstring that does not cross the hip joint
D. Long head of biceps femoris — crossing both the hip and knee joints
E. Adductor magnus (hamstring part) — the only adductor innervated by the tibial nerve

Q5. A 55-year-old man receives an intramuscular injection of an antibiotic into the upper outer quadrant of his right buttock. Two weeks later, he complains of an abnormal gait. On examination, when asked to stand on his right leg, his left pelvis drops significantly. He has no sensory deficit and no weakness of hip flexion, knee extension, knee flexion, or ankle movement. Which nerve has been injured, and which muscle is primarily affected?

A. Superior gluteal nerve — gluteus medius and minimus
B. Inferior gluteal nerve — gluteus maximus
C. Sciatic nerve — hamstrings and muscles below knee
D. Obturator nerve — adductor muscles of the thigh
E. Femoral nerve — quadriceps and sartorius

Q6. An 82-year-old woman falls at home and sustains a displaced intracapsular fracture of the right femoral neck. She undergoes a hemiarthroplasty. The femoral head is sent to pathology and shows avascular necrosis (AVN). Disruption of which blood vessel is primarily responsible for AVN of the femoral head following an intracapsular fracture?

A. Obturator artery — giving off the artery of the ligamentum teres
B. Inferior gluteal artery — supplying the greater trochanter
C. Medial circumflex femoral artery — the main blood supply via retinacular vessels
D. Lateral circumflex femoral artery — anastamosing with the medial circumflex
E. Profunda femoris artery — the deep femoral artery of the thigh

Q7. A 45-year-old warehouse worker presents with a 3-month history of progressive right-sided foot drop. He also reports lower back pain radiating down the lateral aspect of the right leg. On examination, he cannot dorsiflex his right foot or extend his great toe. Sensation is reduced over the lateral leg and dorsum of the foot. However, foot eversion is preserved, and the ankle jerk is intact. Straight leg raise test is positive at 40°. This presentation is MOST consistent with:

A. Common peroneal nerve palsy at the fibular neck — with selective fascicular involvement
B. Sciatic nerve entrapment in the greater sciatic foramen (piriformis syndrome)
C. L5 radiculopathy — from a prolapsed L4/L5 intervertebral disc
D. Deep peroneal nerve entrapment at the ankle (anterior tarsal tunnel syndrome)
E. Tibial nerve compression in the popliteal fossa (Baker's cyst)

Q8. A 22-year-old rugby player is tackled from the lateral side during a match. His right foot is planted on the ground, and his knee sustains a valgus force with external rotation of the tibia. MRI confirms the "unhappy triad" (O'Donoghue's triad). Which three structures are injured?

A. Anterior cruciate ligament + Medial collateral ligament + Medial meniscus
B. Anterior cruciate ligament + Lateral collateral ligament + Lateral meniscus
C. Posterior cruciate ligament + Medial collateral ligament + Medial meniscus
D. Anterior cruciate ligament + Medial collateral ligament + Lateral meniscus
E. Posterior cruciate ligament + Lateral collateral ligament + Lateral meniscus

Key Takeaways

  1. Common peroneal nerve = foot drop (most common lower limb mononeuropathy). Vulnerable at the fibular neck. Fibular neck fracture, plaster cast, leg crossing. Plantarflexion and ankle jerk preserved — distinguishes from sciatic nerve lesion.
  2. Sciatic nerve = foot drop + loss of plantarflexion + absent ankle jerk. Vulnerable in posterior hip dislocation. Common peroneal division more affected (more superficial). Exits greater sciatic foramen below piriformis.
  3. Femoral nerve = weak knee extension + absent knee jerk (L3, L4). Anterior thigh muscles. Vulnerable in pelvic surgery, psoas abscess, femoral artery puncture. Saphenous nerve (sensory) runs all the way to the medial ankle.
  4. Superior gluteal nerve = Trendelenburg gait (pelvis drops on contralateral side). Gluteus medius + minimus. Pure motor. Vulnerable to intramuscular injection if given superomedially.
  5. AVN of femoral head = medial circumflex femoral artery disruption. Intracapsular femoral neck fractures tear retinacular vessels. Artery of ligamentum teres insufficient alone — atrophies with age.
  6. Unhappy triad = ACL + MCL + Medial meniscus. Valgus + external rotation injury. MCL is attached to medial meniscus (LCL is NOT attached to lateral meniscus — classic exam discriminator).
  7. Short head of biceps femoris = only hamstring by common peroneal n. (tibial division supplies all others). Adductor magnus has dual innervation (obturator + tibial). These exceptions are exam favourites.
  8. Popliteal fossa (medial → lateral): Semimembranosus → Artery → Vein → Nerve → Biceps femoris (Serve And Volley Next Ball). Femoral triangle (lateral → medial): NAVEL.
  9. Tarsal tunnel (Tom, Dick, And Very Nervous Harry): Tibialis posterior, flexor Digitorum longus, posterior tibial Artery, tibial Vein, tibial Nerve, flexor Hallucis longus — order at the medial malleolus.
  10. L5 radiculopathy vs common peroneal nerve: L5 = back pain, positive SLR, weak EHL, eversion preserved (L5, S1 via superficial peroneal). CPN = fibular neck tenderness/ trauma, eversion ALSO lost.
