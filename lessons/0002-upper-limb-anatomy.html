Lesson 0002

Upper Limb Anatomy

MRCEM Primary · Phase 1 Foundations · Week 1

🗺️ MRCEM Blueprint Context: Upper Limb Anatomy represents ~8–10 of the 60 Anatomy questions (~5% of total exam). Combined with Lower Limb (~8Q), these two subcategories are the highest-yield anatomy topics. You cannot afford to lose easy marks here — upper limb anatomy questions are highly predictable and reward systematic study.

Why This Matters

Every shift in the Emergency Department brings upper limb pathology: the dislocated shoulder at 2am, the Colles' fracture in the elderly fall, the hand laceration needing a nerve block. You cannot safely perform a shoulder reduction without understanding the axillary nerve. You cannot assess a forearm fracture without knowing which nerve runs with which artery. MRCEM Primary tests anatomy as the foundation of clinical competence — not trivia. This lesson covers the brachial plexus, shoulder, arm, forearm, hand, nerve lesions, and blood supply that the exam expects you to know cold.

Knowledge: The Core Framework

1. The Brachial Plexus — HIGHEST-YIELD TOPIC

The brachial plexus is tested in every MRCEM sitting. It supplies all motor and sensory innervation to the upper limb. Learn it systematically: Roots → Trunks → Divisions → Cords → Branches.

🧠 Mnemonic: Randy Travis Drinks Cold Beer
Roots (C5–T1) → Trunks (Upper, Middle, Lower) → Divisions (Anterior, Posterior) → Cords (Lateral, Posterior, Medial) → Branches

⚡ Cords — named by their relation to the AXILLARY ARTERY:
Lateral cord = lateral to axillary artery (C5, C6, C7 anterior divisions)
Posterior cord = posterior to axillary artery (all posterior divisions: C5–T1)
Medial cord = medial to axillary artery (anterior division of lower trunk: C8, T1)
This is a classic MRCEM question: "The lateral cord of the brachial plexus is formed by…" → answer: anterior divisions of upper and middle trunks.

🧠 Exam pattern: MRCEM frequently tests the root values of specific nerves. Know that C5–C6 = upper trunk lesions affect the axillary, musculocutaneous, and suprascapular nerves (Erb-Duchenne palsy — "waiter's tip" position). C8–T1 = lower trunk lesions affect ulnar nerve and medial part of median nerve (Klumpke's palsy — claw hand). The radial nerve (C5–T1) is the largest terminal branch and can be injured at multiple sites.

2. The Shoulder Joint — Rotator Cuff and Stability

The glenohumeral joint sacrifices stability for mobility. The rotator cuff (SITS muscles) is the primary dynamic stabiliser — it compresses the humeral head into the glenoid fossa.

MuscleRootsNerveActionNotes
SupraspinatusC5, C6Suprascapular n.Initiates abduction (0–15°)Most commonly torn tendon. Runs under coracoacromial arch
InfraspinatusC5, C6Suprascapular n.External rotationPosterior cuff. Power external rotator
Teres minorC5, C6Axillary n.External rotationPosterior. Intact in most massive cuff tears
SubscapularisC5, C6Upper + Lower subscapular n.Internal rotationAnterior cuff. Largest rotator cuff tendon
⚡ Deltoid: NOT a rotator cuff muscle. Innervated by the axillary nerve (C5, C6). Abducts shoulder from 15°–90°. Loss of deltoid → cannot sustain abduction above 15° (supraspinatus can only initiate it). The regimental badge area over the lateral deltoid is the autonomous sensory zone of the axillary nerve — test it after shoulder dislocation.
🧠 ED relevance: Anterior shoulder dislocation can injure the axillary nerve as it passes through the quadrangular space. ALWAYS test the regimental badge sensation and deltoid power before and after reduction. Document it. The axillary nerve wraps around the surgical neck of humerus — also vulnerable in proximal humeral fractures.

3. The Arm — Anterior vs Posterior Compartments

CompartmentMusclesNerveAction
Anterior (Flexor) Biceps brachii, Brachialis, Coracobrachialis Musculocutaneous n. (C5–C7) Elbow flexion + supination (biceps). Brachialis is the prime mover of elbow flexion in all positions.
Posterior (Extensor) Triceps brachii (long, lateral, medial heads) Radial n. (C5–T1) Elbow extension. Long head crosses the shoulder joint.
⚡ High-yield fact: The musculocutaneous nerve pierces the coracobrachialis muscle (its first branch), then runs between biceps and brachialis. Injury → weak elbow flexion (biceps + brachialis) + sensory loss over lateral forearm (lateral cutaneous nerve of forearm).

🔬 Recalled Exam Pattern: The exam loves asking "Which muscle in the anterior forearm is NOT innervated by the median nerve?" The answer is flexor carpi ulnaris (FCU) and the medial half of flexor digitorum profundus (FDP to ring + little fingers) — both supplied by the ulnar nerve. Similarly, "which thenar muscle is innervated by the ulnar nerve?" → adductor pollicis (all other thenar muscles are median). These exceptions are the highest-yield facts in forearm anatomy.

Carpal Tunnel — Contents

The carpal tunnel is a fibro-osseous canal bounded by carpal bones (floor) and the flexor retinaculum (roof). It contains 9 tendons + 1 nerve:

  • 4 tendons of FDS (flexor digitorum superficialis)
  • 4 tendons of FDP (flexor digitorum profundus)
  • 1 tendon of FPL (flexor pollicis longus)
  • Median nerve — the most superficial structure, immediately under the flexor retinaculum

NOT in the carpal tunnel: palmaris longus, FCU, ulnar nerve, ulnar artery, radial artery.

🔥 Quick recall: The carpal tunnel contains how many structures? Which nerve runs through it?
10 structures: 4 FDS tendons + 4 FDP tendons + 1 FPL tendon + median nerve.
The ulnar nerve and artery pass SUPERFICIAL to the flexor retinaculum (through Guyon's canal), NOT through the carpal tunnel.
Palmaris longus (if present) also runs superficial to the retinaculum.

6. Nerve Lesions — THE HIGHEST-YIELD SECTION

MRCEM Primary loves nerve lesion questions. You must know the classic presenting signs, the anatomical level of injury, and the distinguishing features for each nerve.

Cutaneous nerve distribution of the upper limb. Source: Wikimedia Commons.

NerveClassic DeformityMotor LossSensory LossCommon Scenarios
Radial n. Wrist drop Extensors of wrist, fingers, thumb. Triceps spared if injury is at spiral groove (mid-humeral shaft) Posterior arm/forearm, dorsum of hand lateral 3½ fingers (not fingertips) Saturday night palsy (arm draped over chair), humeral shaft fracture, tight plaster cast
Median n. (high) Ape hand / thenar wasting Thenar muscles (LOAF), forearm flexors except FCU + medial FDP. Loss of forearm pronation Lateral 3½ fingers (palmar + dorsal tips) Supracondylar fracture (especially with anterior interosseous branch), carpal tunnel syndrome (low lesion)
Ulnar n. Claw hand (partial — ring + little fingers) Hypothenar, interossei, adductor pollicis, FCU, medial FDP, medial 2 lumbricals Medial 1½ fingers (both palmar + dorsal) Elbow trauma (cubital tunnel), medial epicondyle fracture
Axillary n. Deltoid wasting → loss of shoulder contour Deltoid + teres minor → weak abduction (15–90°) Regimental badge area Anterior shoulder dislocation, proximal humeral fracture (surgical neck)
Musculocutaneous n. Weak elbow flexion Biceps, brachialis, coracobrachialis Lateral forearm (lateral cutaneous n.) Rare in isolation. Usually part of lateral cord / upper trunk injury
Long thoracic n. Winged scapula Serratus anterior → scapula cannot be held against thoracic wall None (pure motor) Axillary surgery (mastectomy + node clearance), heavy rucksack ("rucksack palsy")
⚡ Key discriminator — Claw hand:
Ulnar claw = hyperextension at MCP + flexion at IP of ring + little fingers only. Worse at rest. Ulnar nerve lesion at wrist (low) produces a MORE obvious claw than at elbow (high) — because the FDP to ring/little fingers is also paralysed in a high lesion, reducing the flexor tone that causes the claw.
Median claw = when patient tries to make a fist, index + middle fingers cannot flex (the "hand of benediction" or "preacher's hand"). This is only seen with HIGH median nerve lesions.
🔥 Quick recall: Froment's sign tests which nerve? Describe the test and what a positive result looks like.
Froment's sign tests the ULNAR nerve (specifically adductor pollicis).
The patient holds a piece of paper between thumb and lateral border of index finger. The examiner tries to pull it away.
Positive test: the patient flexes the IP joint of the thumb (using FPL, a median nerve muscle) to compensate for the weak adductor pollicis. This thumb IP flexion is Froment's sign and indicates ulnar nerve palsy.

7. Blood Supply to the Upper Limb

The arterial tree of the upper limb is a continuous vessel that changes name at anatomical landmarks:

Subclavian a. (passes over 1st rib) → Axillary a. (lateral border of 1st rib to inferior border of teres major) → Brachial a. (arm) → bifurcates at the cubital fossa into Radial a. (lateral) + Ulnar a. (medial) → join at the hand as the superficial + deep palmar arches.
⚡ Allen's Test: Compress both radial and ulnar arteries at the wrist, have the patient clench and open their fist repeatedly until the hand blanches. Release ONE artery and observe capillary refill. Repeat for the other artery. This tests patency of the palmar arch and collateral circulation. Essential before radial artery cannulation or harvesting the radial artery for CABG.
🧠 Exam pattern: The exam may ask about the relation of nerves to arteries in the arm. The median nerve crosses anterior to the brachial artery in the cubital fossa (medial to lateral). The ulnar nerve runs with the superior ulnar collateral artery posterior to the medial epicondyle. The radial nerve (posterior interosseous branch) passes through the supinator muscle (arcade of Frohse) — a potential entrapment site.

Skills: Retrieval Practice

Answer each question from memory before clicking. Retrieval builds storage strength. These are constructed to real MRCEM difficulty — clinical vignettes testing nerve localisation and anatomical reasoning, not simple recall.

Q1. A 42-year-old man presents to the Emergency Department unable to extend his right wrist or fingers. He attended a party the previous evening and recalls falling asleep with his right arm draped over the back of a wooden chair. On examination, he has loss of sensation over the dorsum of the right hand and the posterior forearm. Triceps power is preserved. Which nerve has been injured, and at what level?

A. Median nerve — compressed within the carpal tunnel at the wrist
B. Ulnar nerve — compressed in the cubital tunnel at the elbow
C. Radial nerve — compressed against the humerus at the spiral groove
D. Axillary nerve — compressed in the quadrangular space
E. Posterior interosseous nerve — compressed at the arcade of Frohse

Q2. A 52-year-old woman who works as a data entry clerk presents with a 6-month history of nocturnal paraesthesia affecting the thumb, index, middle, and radial half of the ring finger of her right hand. She shakes her hand to relieve the symptoms. On examination, there is wasting of the thenar eminence and weakness of thumb abduction. Tinel's test over the wrist is positive. Which nerve is affected?

A. Median nerve — compressed within the carpal tunnel
B. Ulnar nerve — compressed within Guyon's canal
C. Radial nerve — compressed at the spiral groove
D. Anterior interosseous nerve — compressed in the proximal forearm
E. Posterior interosseous nerve — compressed at the arcade of Frohse

Q3. A 24-year-old motorcyclist is brought to the ED following a high-speed collision. He has an obvious fracture of the middle third of the right clavicle. On neurological examination, he cannot abduct his right shoulder beyond 15° and has markedly weak elbow flexion. Sensation is reduced over the lateral aspect of the upper arm (regimental badge area) and the lateral forearm. This clinical picture is MOST consistent with an injury to which part of the brachial plexus?

A. Upper trunk (C5, C6) — Erb-Duchenne type palsy
B. Middle trunk (C7) — isolated radial nerve territory loss
C. Lower trunk (C8, T1) — Klumpke type palsy
D. Posterior cord — affecting axillary + radial nerves equally
E. Medial cord — ulnar + medial median nerve deficit

Q4. All of the following muscles in the forearm and hand are innervated by the MEDIAN nerve EXCEPT:

A. Pronator teres — the primary pronator of the forearm
B. Abductor pollicis brevis — the most superficial thenar muscle
C. Lateral two lumbricals — flex MCP and extend IP joints of index + middle fingers
D. Flexor carpi ulnaris — the most medial superficial forearm flexor
E. Flexor digitorum superficialis — the intermediate layer flexor of the forearm

Q5. A 16-year-old boy sustains a fracture of the medial epicondyle of the humerus while arm-wrestling. Three months later, he complains of difficulty gripping objects and a change in the appearance of his hand. On examination, there is wasting of the hypothenar eminence, dorsal interosseous guttering, and hyperextension at the metacarpophalangeal joints of the ring and little fingers with flexion at the interphalangeal joints — a "claw hand" deformity. When asked to grip a piece of paper between his thumb and index finger, the examiner can easily pull it away, and the patient flexes the interphalangeal joint of his thumb to compensate. Which nerve is injured?

A. Median nerve — compressed within the carpal tunnel
B. Ulnar nerve — injured at the cubital tunnel behind the medial epicondyle
C. Radial nerve — compressed at the spiral groove of the humerus
D. Anterior interosseous nerve — a pure motor branch of the median nerve
E. Posterior interosseous nerve — the deep terminal branch of the radial nerve

Q6. A 55-year-old recreational tennis player presents with progressive shoulder pain and weakness, particularly when serving. Clinical examination reveals a positive "empty can" test (pain and weakness with resisted shoulder abduction at 90° in the plane of the scapula with thumbs pointing downward). An MRI scan confirms a full-thickness tear of the supraspinatus tendon. Through which space does the supraspinatus tendon pass to insert onto the greater tuberosity of the humerus?

A. Quadrangular space — bounded by teres minor, teres major, long head of triceps, and humerus
B. Triangular space — transmitting the circumflex scapular vessels
C. Subacromial space — between the coracoacromial arch and the humeral head
D. Triangular interval — transmitting the radial nerve and profunda brachii artery
E. Cubital fossa — the triangular depression anterior to the elbow joint

Q7. A 48-year-old woman undergoes a left mastectomy with axillary lymph node clearance for invasive ductal carcinoma. At her 6-week follow-up appointment, she complains that her left shoulder blade "sticks out" when she pushes against a wall. On examination, with the patient facing a wall and pushing with both hands, the medial border of the left scapula is prominently elevated away from the thoracic wall. There is no sensory deficit and no weakness of shoulder abduction or elbow flexion. Which nerve has been injured?

A. Accessory nerve (CN XI) — innervating the trapezius muscle
B. Long thoracic nerve — innervating the serratus anterior muscle
C. Dorsal scapular nerve — innervating the rhomboid muscles
D. Thoracodorsal nerve — innervating the latissimus dorsi muscle
E. Suprascapular nerve — innervating the supraspinatus and infraspinatus

Q8. A 68-year-old man with a history of a left humeral shaft fracture 10 weeks ago (treated conservatively with a plaster cast) is referred because the fracture has united but he cannot extend his wrist or fingers. On examination, he has a complete wrist drop, cannot extend his fingers at the metacarpophalangeal joints, and has sensory loss over the dorsum of the hand and the posterior forearm. However, he CAN extend his elbow normally. Which of the following statements BEST explains why elbow extension is preserved in this radial nerve injury?

A. The triceps is innervated by the axillary nerve, not the radial nerve
B. The branches to the triceps arise from the radial nerve proximal to the spiral groove
C. The triceps receives dual innervation from both the radial nerve and the ulnar nerve
D. The long head of triceps is innervated by the axillary nerve passing through the quadrangular space
E. Triceps function returns first after radial nerve injury due to collateral sprouting

Key Takeaways

  1. Brachial plexus = roots, trunks, divisions, cords, branches (Randy Travis Drinks Cold Beer). Know the root values of every major nerve — this is the single highest-yield anatomy topic.
  2. Radial nerve = wrist drop. Injury at spiral groove spares triceps (branches arise above the groove). Saturday night palsy is a classic ED presentation.
  3. Median nerve = carpal tunnel / ape hand / thenar wasting. Supplies LOAF muscles in the hand. Sensory: lateral 3½ fingers (palmar).
  4. Ulnar nerve = claw hand / Froment's sign. Supplies all interossei, hypothenar muscles, adductor pollicis, FCU, medial FDP. Sensory: medial 1½ fingers (both palmar AND dorsal).
  5. Axillary nerve = deltoid paralysis. Vulnerable in anterior shoulder dislocation and proximal humeral fractures. Test the regimental badge area.
  6. Long thoracic nerve = winged scapula. A pure motor nerve (C5, C6, C7) supplying serratus anterior. Classic post-mastectomy/axillary surgery complication.
  7. Musculocutaneous nerve = anterior compartment of arm. Pierces coracobrachialis, supplies biceps + brachialis, continues as lateral cutaneous nerve of forearm.
  8. Rotator cuff = SITS (Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis). Supraspinatus initiates abduction. Infraspinatus + teres minor = external rotation. Subscapularis = internal rotation.
  9. Anterior forearm exceptions: FCU and medial FDP are ulnar nerve — everything else is median. This "1½ exception" is exam gold.
  10. Allen's test before radial artery cannulation. Tests patency of the palmar arch. Subclavian → axillary → brachial → radial/ulnar.
