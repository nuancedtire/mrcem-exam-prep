Lesson 0001

Cellular Injury, Inflammation & Immune Response

MRCEM Primary · Phase 1 Foundations · Week 1

Why This Matters

Every disease you see in the Emergency Department — from anaphylaxis to myocardial infarction, sepsis to trauma — begins at the cellular level. MRCEM Primary tests your understanding of what happens inside and between cells when injury occurs. This lesson covers roughly 15-20% of the Pathology questions and underpins all of Microbiology.

Knowledge: The Core Framework

1. Cellular Responses to Injury

Cells have four fundamental responses to stress or injury:

ResponseWhat HappensReversible?Key Feature
AdaptationCell changes size/number/type to surviveYesHypertrophy, hyperplasia, atrophy, metaplasia
Reversible injuryCellular swelling, fatty changeYesATP depletion → Na+/K+ pump failure → swelling
Irreversible injuryPoint of no returnNoMitochondrial membrane damage, Ca²⁺ influx
Cell deathNecrosis or apoptosisNoNecrosis = messy; Apoptosis = clean/programmed
🔥 Quick recall: Name the 4 types of cellular adaptation
Hypertrophy — increase in cell SIZE
Hyperplasia — increase in cell NUMBER
Atrophy — decrease in cell size
Metaplasia — change from one mature cell type to another

2. Necrosis vs Apoptosis

This is one of the highest-yield distinctions in MRCEM Primary.

FeatureNecrosisApoptosis
MechanismPathological — energy failureProgrammed — ATP-dependent
Cell sizeSwelling (oncosis)Shrinking (pyknosis)
MembraneRuptures early → leaks contentsIntact until late
InflammationYes — contents trigger itNo — contents contained
NucleusKaryolysis (fading)Karyorrhexis (fragmentation)
PatternGroups of cellsSingle cells
ExamplesMI, stroke, burnEmbryogenesis, viral hepatitis (Councilman bodies)
🧠 Exam pattern: MRCEM loves asking "Which type of cell death is characterized by..." and giving you a description. Memorise the membrane integrity and inflammation rows — those are the most reliable differentiators.

4. Acute Inflammation — The 5 Cardinal Signs

PRISH: Pain, Redness, Immobility, Swelling, Heat

The vascular changes that produce these:

  1. Transient vasoconstriction (seconds)
  2. Vasodilation → redness + heat (histamine, NO, prostaglandins)
  3. Increased permeability → swelling (histamine, bradykinin, leukotrienes)
  4. Leukocyte migration → pain + immobility (margination → rolling → adhesion → diapedesis → chemotaxis)
⚡ Key mediators for the exam:
Histamine — from mast cells. Vasodilation + permeability. The acute-phase mediator.
Prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGI₂) — COX pathway. Vasodilation + pain sensitisation.
Leukotrienes (LTB₄, LTC₄/D₄/E₄) — LOX pathway. Chemotaxis + permeability.
Bradykinin — pain + permeability.
Complement (C3a, C5a) — anaphylatoxins → mast cell degranulation + chemotaxis.

5. Chronic Inflammation

When acute inflammation doesn't resolve. Characterised by:

  • Mononuclear cells (macrophages, lymphocytes, plasma cells) — not neutrophils
  • Tissue destruction
  • Repair (angiogenesis + fibrosis)

Classic example: Granulomatous inflammation — epithelioid macrophages + giant cells (TB, sarcoidosis, Crohn's, fungal infections).

Key Takeaways

  1. Necrosis vs apoptosis = membrane rupture + inflammation vs programmed + contained. Know this cold.
  2. 6 necrosis types — coagulative (MI), liquefactive (brain), caseous (TB), fat (pancreatitis), gangrenous (limb), fibrinoid (vasculitis)
  3. 5 cardinal signs — PRISH. Mediated by histamine, prostaglandins, bradykinin, leukotrienes.
  4. Innate vs adaptive — speed + pattern recognition vs specificity + memory
  5. TLR4 recognises LPS — this is the single most tested TLR fact in MRCEM Primary
